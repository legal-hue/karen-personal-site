About

I was born in Accra. At seventeen, I founded an NGO. At twenty-five, I'd worked across three continents. Now I practise law and build things that matter.

I'm a Barrister at Law, trained at Francis Taylor Building — one of the UK's leading environmental and planning law chambers — and the Government Legal Department. I read Law at the University of Kent, won the Dean's Fellowship to study at UCLA, and completed postgraduate work at SOAS in law, climate change, and natural resource governance.

Before the Bar, I spent years in international development and natural resource governance, including at Save The Children International, where I created and co-chaired Mosaic, a diversity network for BAME employees across the NGO sector. I've written for the Congressional Black Caucus, The Voice, and publications across Ghana on the interplay between law and development on the African continent.

At sixteen, I founded Youth Development Ghana — a project raising awareness of child trafficking and slave labour in the cocoa, fishing, and farming industries. I couldn't wait for someone else to do it. That instinct has shaped everything since.

Today I work across law, consultancy, and writing — always at the intersection of justice, development, and the systems that shape who benefits and who doesn't.
